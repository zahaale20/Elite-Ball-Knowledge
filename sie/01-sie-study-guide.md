# SIE — Securities Industry Essentials: The In-Depth Study Guide

> **Why this exists.** The SIE (Securities Industry Essentials) is FINRA's entry exam — the
> gate you pass *before* a firm sponsors you for a top-off (Series 6, 7, 79, 99, etc.). Most
> prep material is bloated: 400 pages that bury the 20% of facts that produce 80% of the
> questions. This guide inverts that. Every concept is derived from *why the rule exists*, so
> you can reconstruct the answer under pressure instead of memorizing a flashcard you'll forget.
> It is built to **replace** a commercial study book, not supplement it.

> **What passing makes you.** Eligible to be a registered representative once a member firm
> sponsors you and you pass a top-off. The SIE itself does not require sponsorship — anyone 18+
> can sit it, and the result is valid for **four years**.

**Exam mechanics (memorize these first):**

| Fact | Value |
|---|---|
| Scored questions | 75 (plus 10 unscored "pretest" — you can't tell which) |
| Total questions seen | 85 |
| Time | 1 hour 45 minutes (105 min) |
| Passing score | **70%** |
| Cost | ~$80 |
| Validity | 4 years |
| Eligibility | 18+, **no firm sponsorship required** |
| Format | Multiple choice, 4 options, one best answer, computer-based |

**The four sections (weight = where to spend your time):**

| # | Section | Questions | Weight | This guide |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | Knowledge of Capital Markets | 12 | 16% | [§1](#section-1--knowledge-of-capital-markets-16) |

## Section 1 — Knowledge of Capital Markets (16%)

**The first-principle of the whole industry:** capital markets exist to move money from people
who *have* it (investors) to people who can *use* it (issuers — companies and governments), and
to let investors later trade those claims among themselves. Everything else is plumbing and
referees.

### 1.1 The two markets

- **Primary market** — where a security is *created* and sold by the **issuer** to raise capital.
  The issuer gets the money. (IPOs, bond issuance.)
- **Secondary market** — where *existing* securities trade between investors. The issuer gets
  *nothing*; it's investor-to-investor. (NYSE, Nasdaq, OTC.)
- **Why it matters:** the question "who receives the proceeds?" distinguishes them. Primary →
  issuer. Secondary → the selling investor.

### 1.2 The participants (know each role precisely)

| Participant | What they do | Capital at risk? |
|---|---|---|
| **Investor** | Provides capital, bears risk | Yes |
| **Issuer** | Creates/sells securities to raise capital | — |
| **Broker** | **Agent** — executes trades *for* clients, earns a **commission** | No (acts for others) |
| **Dealer** | **Principal** — trades *from its own inventory*, earns a **markup/markdown** | Yes (owns inventory) |
| **Broker-dealer** | A firm doing both | Both |
| **Investment adviser** | Gives advice for a **fee**, fiduciary | — |
| **Municipal advisor** | Advises municipalities on issuance | — |

> **Agent vs. principal is tested constantly.** Agent/broker = commission, acts for the client.
> Principal/dealer = markup, trades for its own account. A firm must disclose its capacity on
> the trade confirmation.

### 1.3 Market centers & systems

- **Exchanges (NYSE, Nasdaq)** — listed markets with listing standards.
- **OTC market** — a *dealer network*, not a physical place; where unlisted securities and
  most bonds trade. The **OTC Bulletin Board** and **OTC Markets (Pink)** quote thin names.
- **Third market** — listed securities traded OTC.
- **Fourth market** — institution-to-institution directly, often via **ECNs** (electronic
  communication networks), bypassing exchanges.
- **Dark pools** — private venues that hide order size to reduce market impact for big
  institutions.

### 1.4 Economic factors the exam loves

- **The business cycle:** expansion → peak → contraction → trough → recovery. A **recession**
  is conventionally **two consecutive quarters of falling GDP**; a prolonged severe one is a
  **depression**.
- **Key indicators:**
  - *Leading* (predict): stock prices, building permits, new orders, money supply.
  - *Coincident* (now): GDP, industrial production, personal income.
  - *Lagging* (confirm): unemployment, corporate profits, CPI.
- **Inflation** = rising prices (measured by **CPI**). **Deflation** = falling prices (worse —
  signals collapsing demand). **Stagflation** = stagnation + inflation together.

### 1.5 The Federal Reserve (monetary policy)

The Fed controls **money and credit**, not spending (that's fiscal/Congress). Its tools:

| Tool | Loosen (stimulate) | Tighten (cool inflation) |
|---|---|---|
| **Open-market operations** (most used) | **Buy** securities → adds reserves | **Sell** securities → drains reserves |
| **Discount rate** (Fed → banks) | Lower | Raise |
| **Reserve requirement** (rarely changed) | Lower | Raise |

- **Fed funds rate** — what banks charge *each other* overnight; the Fed's headline target.
- **Discount rate** — what the Fed charges banks directly.
- **Prime rate** — what banks charge their best corporate customers.
- **Easy money** → lower rates → bond prices up, stimulates economy. **Tight money** → opposite.

### 1.6 International / currency basics

- A **strong dollar** makes imports cheap and US exports expensive (hurts US exporters).
- A **weak dollar** helps US exporters, makes imports pricey.
- **Balance of payments / trade deficit** — flows of money in/out of a country.

## Section 2 — Understanding Products and Their Risks (44%)

This is the exam. Half your studying lives here. Organize products by **the claim they
represent** and **how they pay**, then risk falls out naturally.

### 2.1 Equity securities (ownership)

A share of stock = **ownership** = a residual claim (paid *last*, after all creditors).

**Common stock — the rights:**
1. **Vote** (elect the board; one share = one vote).
   - *Statutory* voting: votes split evenly across seats.
   - *Cumulative* voting: votes can be concentrated — **favors small/minority shareholders**.
2. **Dividends** — *only when declared* by the board; never guaranteed.
3. **Residual claim** in liquidation (last in line).
4. **Inspect** books and **transfer** ownership freely.
5. **Preemptive right** — buy new issues first to avoid dilution (delivered via **rights**).

**Preferred stock — fixed-income-like equity:**
- Pays a **fixed dividend** (stated as % of par, usually $100 par), **priority over common**
  for dividends and liquidation, but **usually no vote**.
- Sensitive to **interest rates** (like a bond) — rates up, preferred price down.
- Types (stack them — they combine):
  - **Cumulative** — skipped dividends accrue and must be paid before common. (vs. *straight/
    non-cumulative* — skipped = gone.)
  - **Participating** — can earn *more* than the stated rate.
  - **Convertible** — exchangeable into common; trades with the common.
  - **Callable** — issuer can redeem (good for issuer, caps your upside).
  - **Adjustable/floating-rate** — dividend tracks a benchmark.

**Other equity instruments:**
- **Rights** — short-term (weeks), let existing holders buy new shares **below market** (from
  the preemptive right). One right per share.
- **Warrants** — **long-term** (years) call-like sweeteners, strike **above** market at
  issuance; often attached to bonds to make them attractive.
- **ADRs (American Depositary Receipts)** — foreign shares trading in the US; carry **currency
  risk**; holders generally **can't vote** and receive dividends in **dollars**.

**Equity risks:** market (systematic), business/financial (unsystematic), liquidity.

### 2.2 Debt securities (lending)

A bond = **a loan**. You are a **creditor**, paid *before* equity. Defined by **par** (usually
$1,000), **coupon** (annual interest), and **maturity**.

**The single most important bond fact — the see-saw:**

> **Interest rates and bond prices move in OPPOSITE directions.** Rates up → existing bond
> prices down (your fixed coupon is now less attractive). This is *interest-rate risk*, and it
> is the most-tested bond concept on the SIE.

**Pricing language:**
- **Premium** — price above par (occurs when coupon > market rate).
- **Discount** — price below par (coupon < market rate).
- **Par** — coupon = market rate.
- Bond points = **$10** (1 point = 1% of $1,000 par). Quoted as % of par (e.g., "98" = $980).

**Yields — and the ranking rule:**
- **Nominal (coupon) yield** — the fixed stated rate.
- **Current yield** = annual coupon ÷ current price.
- **Yield to maturity (YTM)** — total return if held to maturity (includes the pull to par).
- **Yield to call (YTC)** — return if called early.

> **Ranking rules (memorize):**
> - **Discount bond:** Nominal < Current < YTM < YTC (yields rise as you go).
> - **Premium bond:** Nominal > Current > YTM > YTC (yields fall; **call is worst case → "yield
>   to worst"**).

**Bond risks:**
- **Interest-rate risk** — bigger for **long maturity** and **low coupon** (measured by
  *duration*). Zero-coupons are the most rate-sensitive.
- **Credit/default risk** — graded by **Moody's / S&P / Fitch**. **Investment grade** = BBB–/
  Baa3 and up; **junk/high-yield** = below.
- **Reinvestment risk** — can't reinvest coupons at the same rate (zeros avoid this).
- **Call risk** — issuer redeems when rates fall, right when you'd want to keep the high coupon.
- **Purchasing-power (inflation) risk** — fixed payments lose value; worst for long bonds.

**Corporate debt ladder (seniority in bankruptcy):**
1. Secured (mortgage bonds, equipment trust certificates)
2. Senior unsecured **debentures** (backed only by issuer's *full faith*)
3. Subordinated debentures
4. Then preferred stock, then common (equity is last).

- **Income/adjustment bonds** — pay interest only if earned (risky).
- **Convertible bonds** — exchangeable into stock; lower coupon in exchange for upside.
- **Commercial paper** — short-term (≤270 days) unsecured corporate note, issued at a discount,
  **exempt** from registration.

**US Government & agency:**
- **T-bills** — ≤52 weeks, **discount** instrument (no coupon), most liquid, virtually no
  default risk.
- **T-notes** — 2–10 yr, semiannual coupon.
- **T-bonds** — 10–30 yr, semiannual coupon.
- **TIPS** — principal adjusts with CPI (inflation protection).
- **STRIPS** — government **zero-coupons**.
- Treasuries are **state-tax-free**, federally taxable.
- **Agencies:** **GNMA (Ginnie Mae)** is the only one **backed by the full faith and credit**
  of the US government; **FNMA/FHLMC** are not (implied only). Mortgage-backed → **prepayment
  risk**.

**Municipal bonds (issued by state/local governments):**
- **Tax-exempt** at the federal level (the defining feature) — and "triple-tax-free" if you
  live in the issuing state.
- Therefore suited to **high-tax-bracket** investors. Compare via **tax-equivalent yield** =
  muni yield ÷ (1 − tax bracket).
- Two flavors:
  - **GO (general obligation)** — backed by **taxing power**; needs **voter approval**.
  - **Revenue** — backed by a specific project's **revenue** (toll road, stadium); no voter
    approval; riskier (depends on the project).
- A muni is **inappropriate inside an IRA/401(k)** — you'd waste the tax exemption.

**Money-market instruments (short-term, ≤1 yr, low risk):** T-bills, commercial paper,
negotiable CDs (≥$100k), banker's acceptances (trade finance), repos.

### 2.3 Packaged products / investment companies

Governed by the **Investment Company Act of 1940**. Three legal types:

1. **Face-amount certificate companies** (rare).
2. **Unit Investment Trusts (UITs)** — **fixed**, unmanaged portfolio; redeemable units; no
   board, no investment adviser.
3. **Management companies** — actively/passively managed; split into:
   - **Open-end (mutual fund):** issues **unlimited** shares, **continuously offered and
     redeemable** by the fund at **NAV** (forward pricing — next computed NAV). No secondary
     market. Priced once daily.
   - **Closed-end fund:** **fixed** number of shares sold in an IPO, then **trades on an
     exchange** at a market price that can be at a **premium or discount to NAV**.

**Mutual fund share classes (a fee question):**
- **Class A** — front-end load (pay sales charge up-front); lower ongoing 12b-1 fees; **breakpoints** (volume discounts).
- **Class B** — back-end load / **CDSC** (contingent deferred sales charge that declines to
  zero over years); higher 12b-1; usually convert to A eventually.
- **Class C** — level load; high ongoing 12b-1; no/low front or back end; worst for **long**
  holds.

> **Breakpoint sale = a violation.** Steering a client *just below* a breakpoint to earn a
> higher commission is prohibited. A **letter of intent (LOI)** (13-month look-back/forward)
> and **rights of accumulation** let investors reach breakpoints.

- **NAV** = (assets − liabilities) ÷ shares outstanding. **POP (public offering price)** =
  NAV + sales charge. Max sales charge under FINRA = **8.5%** (with conditions).
- **12b-1 fees** — annual fees for distribution/marketing, taken from fund assets.

**ETFs (exchange-traded funds):**
- Hold a basket (often an index) but **trade intraday on an exchange like a stock** — can be
  bought on margin and sold short. Low cost, tax-efficient.
- **Leveraged/inverse ETFs** — use derivatives to multiply or invert daily returns; **reset
  daily**, so they're **unsuitable for long-term holds** (tracking decay). Heavily tested as a
  suitability trap.

**Other pooled products:**
- **REITs** — own/finance real estate; must distribute **≥90%** of taxable income to
  shareholders; trade like stock; **not** investment companies; dividends generally **don't**
  qualify for the lower qualified-dividend rate. Types: equity, mortgage, hybrid.
- **Hedge funds** — private, illiquid, leveraged, for **accredited/qualified** investors;
  lightly regulated; high fees ("2 and 20").
- **DPPs (direct participation programs / limited partnerships)** — **flow through** income/
  losses to investors (no entity-level tax); illiquid; used for real estate, oil & gas. A
  **limited partner** has limited liability and no management role; the **general partner**
  manages and has unlimited liability.

### 2.4 Options (derivatives)

An option is a **contract**, not ownership. Standard contract = **100 shares**. Defined by
**strike**, **premium** (the price), and **expiration**.

- **Call** — right to **buy** at the strike. Buyer is **bullish**; wants the stock to **rise**.
- **Put** — right to **sell** at the strike. Buyer is **bearish**; wants the stock to **fall**.

> **Mnemonic:** **Call Up, Put Down** (what the *buyer* wants). The **seller/writer** wants the
> opposite and keeps the premium.

| | Wants stock to… | Max gain | Max loss |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Long call** (buyer) | Rise | Unlimited | Premium paid |
| **Short call** (writer) | Stay/fall | Premium | **Unlimited** (uncovered) |
| **Long put** (buyer) | Fall | Strike − premium (large) | Premium paid |
| **Short put** (writer) | Stay/rise | Premium | Strike − premium |

- **In/at/out of the money:** a **call** is *in the money* when stock **> strike**; a **put**
  is in the money when stock **< strike**. Intrinsic value never negative.
- **Premium = intrinsic value + time value.** At expiration, only intrinsic value remains.
- **Covered call** — writing a call against stock you own; generates income, caps upside.
- **Protective put** — buying a put on stock you own; portfolio **insurance** (hedge).
- Options are governed by the **OCC (Options Clearing Corporation)**; every buyer must receive
  the **OCC Options Disclosure Document (ODD)** at or before account approval.

### 2.5 Other products

- **Annuities** (insurance products):
  - **Fixed annuity** — insurer bears investment risk; guaranteed payout; **not a security**
    (state-insurance regulated).
  - **Variable annuity** — owner bears investment risk via **subaccounts**; **is a security**
    (and an insurance product); tax-deferred growth; needs prospectus. Sales abuses
    (high fees, unsuitable for elderly/short horizons) are a recurring exam theme.
  - **Accumulation vs. annuitization (payout) phase.** **Annuitization is irrevocable.**
- **529 plans / Coverdell ESAs** — education savings; 529 has high limits, state tax perks.
- **Structured products, ETNs** — debt of an issuer linked to an index; carry **issuer credit
  risk**.

### 2.6 The risk vocabulary (be able to classify any risk)

| Risk | One-line definition |
|---|---|
| **Systematic / market** | Whole-market moves; **cannot be diversified away** (hedge instead) |
| **Unsystematic / business** | Specific to one company/sector; **diversifiable** |
| **Interest-rate** | Bond/preferred prices fall as rates rise |
| **Credit / default** | Issuer fails to pay |
| **Liquidity / marketability** | Can't sell quickly at fair price |
| **Inflation / purchasing-power** | Fixed income loses real value |
| **Reinvestment** | Can't reinvest cash flows at the old rate |
| **Call / prepayment** | Issuer redeems early when rates fall |
| **Political / legislative / regulatory** | Law or policy change hurts value |
| **Currency / exchange-rate** | Foreign holdings hurt by FX moves |
| **Capital** | You can lose the money you invested |

> **Diversification reduces *unsystematic* risk only.** Systematic risk is reduced by
> **hedging** (e.g., buying puts) or asset allocation — never by diversifying within one asset
> class. This distinction is a guaranteed question.

## Section 3 — Trading, Customer Accounts & Prohibited Activities (31%)

### 3.1 Order types & trade lifecycle

- **Market order** — execute **immediately** at best available price; guarantees **execution**,
  not price.
- **Limit order** — execute only at the limit price **or better**; guarantees **price**, not
  execution. *Buy limit* fills at the limit or lower; *sell limit* at the limit or higher.
- **Stop (stop-loss) order** — dormant until a **trigger** price, then becomes a market order.
  Used to limit loss or protect profit.
- **Stop-limit** — becomes a *limit* order when triggered (price protection, execution risk).

> **Placement logic (tested):** *Sell stops* and *buy limits* sit **below** the market;
> *buy stops* and *sell limits* sit **above** the market.

- **Settlement (Reg T / industry):** Regular-way for stocks/corporates/munis is **T+1** (moved
  from T+2 in May 2024). Options and government securities settle **T+1**. Cash settlement =
  same day.
- **Bid/ask:** customers **buy at the ask**, **sell at the bid**; the difference is the
  **spread** (dealer's compensation). **Inside market** = highest bid + lowest ask.

### 3.2 Margin accounts (Reg T)

Buying with borrowed money. Governed by the Fed's **Regulation T** (currently **50%** initial
requirement) and FINRA maintenance rules.

- **Initial (Reg T):** deposit **50%** of the purchase; broker lends the rest.
- **Minimum maintenance (FINRA):** **25%** for long positions, **30%** for short.
- **Long market value − debit balance = equity.** A **margin call** comes when equity falls
  below maintenance.
- **Minimums:** $2,000 to open (or 100% if the purchase is smaller).
- Customer must sign a **margin agreement** (credit + hypothecation; loan-consent is optional).
- **Risk:** margin **magnifies both gains and losses**; you can lose **more than you invested**
  on a short. Not all securities are marginable (e.g., options can't be bought on margin;
  new issues restricted for ~30 days).

### 3.3 Customer accounts

**Account registration types:**
- **Individual** — one owner.
- **Joint:**
  - **JTWROS (joint tenants with right of survivorship)** — on death, share passes to the
    survivor(s).
  - **Tenants in common (TIC)** — on death, share passes to the **estate**, not the co-owner.
- **Custodial (UGMA/UTMA)** — one minor, one custodian; **irrevocable gift**; minor's SSN;
  account belongs to the minor at majority.
- **Trust, estate, corporate, partnership** — need the governing documents.
- **Discretionary** — rep can trade **without prior approval each time**; requires **written
  authorization** and **principal review**. Discretion = deciding **A**sset, **A**ction, or
  **A**mount. (Price/time alone is *not* discretion.)
- **Fee-based vs. commission-based** — suitability of the account type itself matters.

**Death of a customer:** cancel open orders, freeze the account, await legal docs (death
certificate, court letters); margin and trading stop.

**Required at account opening:**
- **Customer Identification Program (CIP)** — name, DOB, address, **SSN/TIN** (USA PATRIOT Act).
- A **new account form**; the customer's **signature is not required to open** (but is for
  margin/options).
- **Suitability / Reg BI** info: financial status, tax status, investment objectives, risk
  tolerance, time horizon, liquidity needs, experience.

### 3.4 Regulation Best Interest (Reg BI) & suitability

- **Reg BI (2020)** raised the standard for broker-dealers to act in the customer's **best
  interest** at the time of a recommendation — higher than old "suitability," not quite the
  adviser's full fiduciary duty.
- Four obligations: **Disclosure, Care, Conflict-of-interest, Compliance.**
- **Form CRS (Customer Relationship Summary)** must be delivered to retail customers — plain-
  English explanation of services, fees, conflicts, standard of conduct.

### 3.5 Prohibited activities (the integrity core — heavily tested)

| Violation | What it is |
|---|---|
| **Churning** | Excessive trading to generate commissions, not for the client's benefit |
| **Front-running** | Trading ahead of a known large customer order |
| **Insider trading** | Trading on material, non-public information (MNPI) — **Insider Trading & Securities Fraud Enforcement Act of 1988**; penalties up to **3× profit gained/loss avoided** |
| **Markup/markdown abuse** | Excessive, undisclosed dealer compensation (FINRA 5% policy — a *guideline*, not a hard cap) |
| **Selling away** | Selling securities not offered through your firm without firm approval |
| **Painting the tape / matched orders** | Faking volume/activity to manipulate price |
| **Pump and dump** | Inflating a stock with false hype then selling |
| **Spoofing/layering** | Fake orders to move price |
| **Freeriding** | Buying then selling without paying (Reg T violation → 90-day freeze) |
| **Commingling** | Mixing customer and firm assets |
| **Guaranteeing against loss** | Prohibited — you may **never** promise a customer won't lose |
| **Sharing in accounts** | Only with written approval and **proportional** to your own contribution |
| **Backdating / falsifying records** | Prohibited |
| **Unauthorized trading** | Trading without authority/discretion |

**AML (Anti-Money Laundering — Bank Secrecy Act / USA PATRIOT Act):**
- Three stages: **Placement → Layering → Integration.**
- **CTR (Currency Transaction Report):** cash > **$10,000** in a day.
- **SAR (Suspicious Activity Report):** suspicious activity ≥ **$5,000**; **do not tip off** the
  customer.
- **Structuring** (splitting cash to dodge the $10k threshold) is itself a crime.
- **OFAC** — screen against the **SDN** sanctioned-persons list.

### 3.6 Communications with the public (FINRA Rule 2210)

Three categories — the distinction is **audience + number of people**:

| Type | Definition | Approval |
|---|---|---|
| **Retail communication** | Distributed to **>25 retail** investors in 30 days (ads, websites, mailers) | **Principal pre-approval**; some filed with FINRA |
| **Correspondence** | To **≤25 retail** investors in 30 days | Supervised/reviewed (post-use sampling OK) |
| **Institutional communication** | To **institutional** investors only | Supervised, no principal pre-approval |

- **Standards:** fair, balanced, **not misleading**; **no guarantees**, no exaggerated/
  unwarranted claims, no predicting performance; past performance ≠ future results.
- **Testimonials** require disclosures; **no cherry-picked** recommendations without context.

## Section 4 — Overview of the Regulatory Framework (9%)

### 4.1 The landmark laws (know what each one *did*)

| Law | What it created / governs |
|---|---|
| **Securities Act of 1933** | The **primary market** — **registration & prospectus** for new issues ("paper act"; full disclosure) |
| **Securities Exchange Act of 1934** | The **secondary market** — created the **SEC**, regulates exchanges, broker-dealers, insider trading, margin |
| **Maloney Act of 1938** | Authorized **SROs** for the OTC market (led to NASD → FINRA) |
| **Investment Company Act of 1940** | Regulates mutual funds / investment companies |
| **Investment Advisers Act of 1940** | Registers and regulates **investment advisers** |
| **Securities Investor Protection Act (SIPC) 1970** | Protects customers if a **broker-dealer fails** |
| **Insider Trading Act of 1988** | Penalties (3×) for trading on MNPI |
| **USA PATRIOT Act 2001** | AML, CIP requirements |
| **Sarbanes-Oxley 2002** | Corporate governance, financial-reporting integrity |
| **Dodd-Frank 2010** | Post-2008 reform; municipal advisor registration; whistleblower program |
| **JOBS Act 2012** | Eased capital-raising (crowdfunding, emerging-growth companies) |

### 4.2 The regulators & SROs

- **SEC** — the **federal** top regulator (a government agency). Oversees the SROs.
- **FINRA** — the largest **SRO** (self-regulatory organization); writes/enforces broker-dealer
  conduct rules, runs the exams and **CRD/BrokerCheck**.
- **MSRB** — writes rules for **municipal** securities (but does **not** enforce them — FINRA/
  SEC enforce).
- **CBOE / OCC** — options. **FRB** — margin (Reg T). **State regulators** — **Blue-Sky** laws
  (registration at the state level; **Uniform Securities Act**).
- **SIPC** — insurance-like protection if a **firm fails**: up to **$500,000** per customer,
  of which max **$250,000** in cash. (Covers missing securities/cash from failure — **not**
  market losses.)

### 4.3 Registration & the SIE-to-RR path

- **The SIE** tests product/market/regulatory basics and needs **no sponsorship**.
- A **representative-level top-off** (Series 6, 7, 79, 22, 99…) + **firm sponsorship** + the SIE
  = a registered representative.
- **Form U4** — registers/onboards a rep (discloses background, fingerprints, disclosures).
- **Form U5** — files **termination** within **30 days**; the firm must report the reason.
- **Statutory disqualification** — felonies/securities misdemeanors in the past **10 years**,
  certain regulatory bars, can bar association with a firm.
- **Fingerprinting** required for associated persons.

### 4.4 Continuing education & conduct

- **CE:** **Regulatory Element** (now **annual**) + **Firm Element** (firm-designed training).
- **Outside business activity (OBA)** — must give **prior written notice** to the firm.
- **Private securities transactions / selling away** — need **prior written approval**.
- **Gifts** — capped at **$100 per person per year**. **Political contributions** — MSRB G-37
  **pay-to-play** limits ($250 per election to officials you can vote for) to keep muni business
  clean.
- **Borrowing/lending with customers** — prohibited unless narrow exceptions + firm policy.

## How to study this (the meta-strategy)

1. **Front-load Section 2.** It's 44% and the densest. If products feel automatic, you pass.
2. **Drill the binary distinctions** that the exam weaponizes: agent vs. principal, primary vs.
   secondary, GO vs. revenue, open- vs. closed-end, call vs. put, market vs. limit, JTWROS vs.
   TIC, systematic vs. unsystematic, Reg T (50%) vs. maintenance (25/30%).
3. **Learn the *why*, not the flashcard.** "Rates up → bond prices down" because your fixed
   coupon is now worth less than new issues. Derive it and you can't forget it.
4. **Practice questions are the real studying.** Read this guide once for the model, then live
   in the [question bank](02-sie-rapid-review-and-question-bank.md). Aim for **85%+** on
   practice before sitting (target is 70%, build margin).
5. **Two days before:** review only the [rapid-review sheet](02-sie-rapid-review-and-question-bank.md#rapid-review-the-night-before)
   and the numbers table below.

> **One-paragraph summary of the whole exam.** Capital markets move money from investors to
> issuers (primary) and let investors trade afterward (secondary), refereed by the SEC and SROs
> like FINRA. Securities are either **ownership** (stock — paid last, votes, dividends) or
> **loans** (bonds — paid first, fixed coupon, price moves opposite to rates), with **packaged**
> products (funds, ETFs, annuities) and **derivatives** (options — calls up, puts down) layered
> on top. Each carries a nameable risk. Customers open accounts under rules (Reg T margin, Reg
> BI best interest, CIP/AML), reps must avoid a fixed list of prohibited acts (churning, insider
> trading, selling away), and a stack of laws from **1933/1934** onward defines who may do what.
> Know the binary distinctions and the numbers table, and the 70% is yours.
