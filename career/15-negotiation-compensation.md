# Compensation & Negotiation for Defense-Tech Engineers

> A standalone companion to [11-career-defense-aerospace-playbook.md](11-defense-aerospace-playbook.md).
> The playbook gets you the offer. This file gets you paid what the offer is
> actually worth — and helps you choose the *right* offer, not just the biggest
> number.

The single highest-ROI hour of your career is the one you spend negotiating an
offer. There is no other moment where five focused emails move five-figure sums.
Most engineers underperform here purely from discomfort, not lack of leverage.

---

## 1. Understand the Three Pay Worlds

Defense/aerospace spans three very different compensation cultures. Know which
one you're negotiating against — the levers are different.

| World | Examples | Comp shape | Negotiation reality |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Defense-tech startups** | Anduril, Shield AI, Saronic, Applied Intuition | Base + meaningful **equity** + bonus | Equity is the real lever; bands are flexible early |
| **Primes** | Lockheed, RTX, Northrop, Boeing, GD | Base + bonus, **pension/benefits**, little/no equity | Banded by job code; less flex, but relocation/sign-on negotiable |
| **Big Tech (adjacent)** | for comparison/leverage | Base + large **RSUs** + bonus | Sets your outside-option number |

Your leverage comes from being credible in more than one world at once.

---

## 2. The Components You're Actually Negotiating

Never negotiate "salary." Negotiate the **package**. Each lever has different
flexibility:

- **Base salary** — least flexible at primes (job-code banded), more flexible at
  startups. Anchors future raises, so a dollar here compounds.
- **Equity** — the big one at startups. Negotiate **number of shares / RSU
  value**, not just "equity." Ask: strike price, current 409A / preferred price,
  total shares outstanding (to compute your %), vesting schedule, cliff.
- **Sign-on bonus** — the easiest "yes." Used to bridge a gap when base is
  capped. Often has a clawback if you leave within 12 months.
- **Annual / target bonus** — % of base; ask about historical payout vs. target.
- **Refresh / follow-on grants** — what happens at year 2–4? A great initial
  grant with no refreshers is a cliff.
- **Relocation** — lump sum vs. managed move; negotiable and often forgotten.
- **Start date, PTO, remote flexibility, clearance sponsorship** — non-cash
  levers that cost the company little but matter a lot to you.

---

## 3. Equity, Demystified

Startup equity is where the life-changing money — and the most confusion — lives.

- **% ownership** = your shares ÷ fully-diluted shares outstanding. Always ask
  for the denominator. "10,000 shares" is meaningless without it.
- **ISO vs. NSO vs. RSU** — ISOs (incentive stock options) have tax advantages
  but AMT risk; NSOs are taxed as income at exercise; RSUs (later-stage) are
  taxed at vesting. Know which you're getting.
- **Strike price & 409A** — options let you *buy* at the strike. Your paper gain
  is (current value − strike) × shares. A low strike on an appreciating company
  is the dream.
- **Vesting** — standard is 4 years, 1-year cliff, monthly after. No cliff means
  you vest from month one (rare, valuable).
- **Preferred vs. common** — investors hold preferred (gets paid first in a
  sale). You hold common. In a mediocre exit, common can be worth far less than
  the headline valuation implies (liquidation preferences).
- **Dilution** — future funding rounds shrink your %. Not inherently bad if the
  pie grows faster than your slice shrinks.
- **The honest framing:** value startup equity as a *lottery ticket with
  positive expected value*, not as guaranteed cash. Take an offer you'd be happy
  with **if the equity went to zero**.

## 4. The Negotiation Sequence

1. **Never name a number first.** "I'm focused on finding the right fit; I'm
   confident we can land on a fair package once we know it's a match." If pushed,
   give a *range* anchored on research, with your target at the bottom.
2. **Get the offer in writing first.** You negotiate against a written offer, not
   a hallway conversation.
3. **Express genuine enthusiasm, then negotiate.** "I'm really excited about this
   — I want to make it work. Can we talk about the package?" Enthusiasm + a
   specific ask is the winning combination.
4. **Anchor high but defensibly.** Tie every ask to data (market comp, competing
   offer, scope of role).
5. **Negotiate the whole package, ranked.** Lead with your top priority. If base
   is capped, pivot to sign-on or equity.
6. **Use competing offers honestly.** A real competing offer is the strongest
   lever in existence. Never bluff one — it's checkable and ends trust.
7. **Get the final deal in writing before you resign anything.**

---

## 5. Scripts That Work

**The counter (email):**
> Thank you — I'm genuinely excited about the team and the mission. Based on my
> research for this scope and my background in [X], I was targeting a base closer
> to $___. Is there flexibility to get there, or to bridge the gap with a sign-on
> or additional equity? I'm confident we can make this work.

**The competing-offer leverage:**
> I want to be transparent: I have another offer at $___ total. You're my first
> choice because of [specific reason]. If we can get closer on [lever], this is
> an easy yes for me.

**The "is this your best" close:**
> This is close. If you can do [specific ask], I'll sign today.

**When they say the band is fixed:**
> I understand base is banded. Given that, can we look at the sign-on bonus,
> equity refresh timing, or start date / relocation to close the gap?

## 6. Clearance & Defense-Specific Factors

- **An active clearance is worth real money** — it saves the employer 6–18
  months and tens of thousands in investigation cost. If you hold one (or are in
  process), it's a legitimate negotiating point.
- **Sponsorship timing matters.** If you need sponsorship, clarify whether the
  start date / role depends on adjudication, and whether there's interim work.
- **Primes vs. startups on clearance:** primes are deeply experienced at
  sponsoring; some startups are newer to it. Ask directly.
- **Polygraph-required roles** (some IC work) carry premiums and longer onboarding.

---

## 7. Mistakes That Cost the Most

- **Accepting on the spot.** Always ask for time: "Thank you — can I have a few
  days to review?" No reasonable employer rescinds for this.
- **Negotiating before the offer.** No leverage until they've chosen you.
- **Anchoring on current salary.** Negotiate on market value and scope, not your
  last paycheck. (In many U.S. states, employers can't even ask.)
- **Forgetting equity math.** Comparing a startup's "shares" to base salary
  without the denominator and strike price.
- **Bluffing offers.** One discovered bluff destroys all trust and leverage.
- **Ignoring non-cash levers.** PTO, remote flexibility, start date, and growth
  scope compound over years.
- **Going silent after the counter.** Negotiation is a conversation; stay warm.

---

## 8. After You Sign

- **Re-negotiation happens at performance reviews and on competing offers.** Keep
  a running "brag doc" of shipped impact — it's your evidence at raise time.
- **Equity refreshes** are negotiated, not automatic. Bring it up before year 4.
- **Get promoted on scope, not tenure.** Document the next level's
  responsibilities you already perform.

## 9. Total Comp, Decomposed: What Each Dollar Is Worth

"Total comp" (TC) is the only number that matters when you compare offers. But not
all dollars inside it are equal — they differ in *certainty*, *timing*, and *tax*.
Build the habit of decomposing every offer into the same five buckets so you can
compare a prime, a startup, and a Big Tech offer on one ruler.

| Bucket | Certainty | When you get it | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Base salary** | High (contractual) | Every paycheck | The floor you can plan your life on. Anchors raises and your next job's number. |
| **Target/annual bonus** | Medium | Yearly, after review | Quoted as % of base (10–20% common). Ask actual payout-vs-target history. |
| **Equity** | Low→High by stage | Over 4 yrs (vesting) | RSUs (liquid, late-stage) ≠ options (a bet). The wildcard. |
| **Sign-on bonus** | High (one-time) | Up front | Bridges gaps; usually clawed back if you leave <12 mo. |
| **Clearance / skill premiums** | High | Built into base or stipend | An active TS/SCI or niche skill (RF, GNC, embedded RT) is a line item. |

**The mental model:** weight each dollar by the odds you actually receive it.
A $250K startup TC that is 60% illiquid options at a Series A is *not* comparable
to a $250K prime TC that is 90% cash. Both can be the right choice — but only if
you priced them honestly.

> Rule of thumb for the drone-autonomy path: if you're optimizing for *learning
> velocity and ownership* (the thing that makes you worth more in five years),
> weight early-stage equity and scope. If you're optimizing for *stability while
> you build clearance and a track record*, weight base and benefits. See
> [19-career-leadership-growth.md](19-leadership-growth.md) for the
> long-game framing.

## 10. Leveling & Bands — Mapping Titles to Money

Every employer pays a *band* (a min–mid–max range) tied to an internal **level**,
not to your title. Your single biggest comp lever after the offer is **what level
they slot you into** — a level bump is worth more than any within-band haggle.

### How levels map (illustrative, verify current data)
| Rough career stage | Big Tech ladder | Defense-tech startup | Prime (job code) |
|---|---|---|---|
| New grad / E2 | L3 / SDE I | Member of Technical Staff | Engineer I–II |
| Solid mid (3–5 yr) | L4 / SDE II | Senior MTS | Engineer III / Sr. Engineer |
| Senior (owns systems) | L5 / Senior | Staff | Principal / Lead |
| Staff (owns a domain) | L6 / Staff | Senior Staff | Senior Principal |

### The levers inside leveling
- **Fight the level, not the band.** "Based on the systems I've owned —
  PX4/Pixhawk autonomy stack, SITL test harness, onboard compute integration —
  the scope you described is a Senior role. Can we align on level before we talk
  numbers?" A level change moves base *and* equity *and* future raises.
- **Bands are public-ish.** levels.fyi and Glassdoor leak the boundaries. Walk in
  knowing the band's midpoint; ask for mid-to-upper, never the floor.
- **Primes are the most rigid** — comp is locked to a GS-like job code and grade.
  There the move is title/grade at the *requisition* stage, plus sign-on and
  relocation, not base.
- **Down-leveling is the silent tax.** If they slot you a level below your scope,
  every future raise compounds off a smaller base. Push hard here even if it
  delays the offer a week.

Cross-reference your evidence with [18-career-resume-portfolio.md](18-resume-portfolio.md)
(the artifacts that prove scope) and [17-career-interview-prep.md](17-interview-prep.md)
(how the interview loop sets your level in the first place).

## 11. How to Research the Real Numbers

You cannot anchor on data you don't have. Spend a focused afternoon *before* the
recruiter ever asks about money.

### The data stack
1. **levels.fyi** — best for Big Tech and a growing set of defense-tech firms.
   Filter by company → level → location. Read the *median and the spread*, not
   one heroic data point.
2. **Glassdoor / Blind** — noisier, but Blind ("teamblind") has candid threads on
   Anduril, SkyDio, Shield AI offer ranges and negotiation outcomes.
3. **levels.fyi "Internal" / H1B disclosure data** — public LCA filings show real
   base salaries primes and startups paid sponsored workers. Searchable via
   h1bdata.info / myvisajobs. Base only, but it's *ground truth*.
4. **OPM GS tables** — for any prime/federal-adjacent role, the GS scale plus
   locality pay sets the gravity well the band orbits.
5. **Your network** — one honest conversation with someone one level above you at
   the target company beats every website. Ask ranges, not exact figures:
   "What's the band for a Staff role here, roughly?"

### Triangulate, then set three numbers
- **Walk-away (your floor / reservation price):** below this you decline.
- **Target (realistic, well-supported):** what you'll actually push for.
- **Anchor (ambitious but defensible):** the first number you'd float *if forced*,
  always above target so the meet-in-the-middle lands near target.

Write these down before any call. Negotiation is mostly the discipline of not
abandoning numbers you set with a clear head.

## 12. BATNA & Leverage — The Theory Underneath the Scripts

Every script in Section 5 is an application of one idea: **leverage comes from
your alternatives, not your needs.**

- **BATNA** = *Best Alternative To a Negotiated Agreement* — what you'll do if this
  deal falls through. A strong BATNA (a competing offer, a job you're happy to
  keep, runway in the bank) is the source of all calm and all power.
- **Build your BATNA on purpose.** Run interview loops in parallel so offers land
  in the same two-week window. One offer is a salary; two offers is a market.
- **Their BATNA matters too.** A company that just spent six weeks and five
  interviewers on you has a *bad* alternative (start over). That sunk cost is
  quiet leverage in your favor — use it gently, never smugly.
- **ZOPA** = *Zone Of Possible Agreement* — the overlap between their max and your
  min. Good negotiation finds and splits the ZOPA; it doesn't try to win.
- **Never reveal your floor.** The moment they know your walk-away, the ZOPA
  collapses onto it. Reveal your *target as a range*, never your reservation price.

> Defense-tech reality: your clearance status, a published autonomy portfolio, and
> a credible competing offer from a *different* world (a Big Tech RSU offer next
> to an Anduril equity offer) are three independent sources of BATNA. Stack them.
> See [11-career-defense-aerospace-playbook.md](11-defense-aerospace-playbook.md)
> for generating multiple offers, and [16-career-security-clearance.md](16-security-clearance.md)
> for why an active clearance is leverage.

## 13. More Scripts for the Hard Moments

**The recruiter asks for your number on the first call:**
> "I'd rather not anchor on a number before we both know it's a fit — I'm sure
> you'll make a competitive offer for the level. What's the band you have budgeted
> for this role?" *(Deflect, then make them go first.)*

**They ask your current salary (where legal):**
> "My current comp isn't really representative of the market for this scope, so I'm
> focused on what's fair for the role. What range are you working with?"

**You only have one offer but want to negotiate:**
> "This is my top choice and I want to make it work today. Based on my research for
> a [level] role with this scope, I was targeting $___ total. Can we close that
> gap on base, sign-on, or equity?" *(No bluff needed — data is its own leverage.)*

**Exploding offer / artificial deadline:**
> "I'm genuinely excited and want to give this the serious decision it deserves.
> I have one more conversation scheduled this week — can we extend to [date]? I
> don't want to rush a yes I'm fully committed to." *(Most deadlines are soft.)*

**They counter your counter and it's close:**
> "That's nearly there. If you can do [specific final ask], I'll commit and sign
> this week." *(Name the exact thing that turns it into a yes.)*

**Renegotiating a refresh / raise internally:**
> "Here's the brag doc of what I've shipped this year against the next level's
> scope. I'd like to talk about an equity refresh and a level review at this
> cycle." *(Evidence first, ask second.)*

**Declining gracefully without burning the bridge:**
> "I've decided to go another direction this time, but I was genuinely impressed
> by the team and the mission — I'd love to stay in touch." *(Defense-tech is
> small; today's no is next year's offer.)*

## 14. Equity Refreshers, Cliffs & the Year-4 Trap

Initial grants get all the attention; **refreshers** are where multi-year wealth
is actually won or lost — and almost nobody negotiates them up front.

- **The year-4 cliff.** A four-year grant fully vests at year four and then your
  equity income *drops to zero* unless refreshers were stacking. Strong companies
  grant annual refreshers starting year 2–3 so vesting overlaps. Ask: *"What's
  the typical refresh cadence and size for someone performing at this level?"*
- **Front-loaded vs. back-loaded vesting.** Standard is 25/25/25/25. Some firms
  back-load (5/15/40/40) to handcuff you. Read the schedule, not the headline.
- **Double-trigger acceleration.** For startup RSUs, you want vesting to trigger
  on *both* an acquisition *and* your termination — protects you in an exit.
- **Liquidation preferences & participation.** In a soft exit, preferred holders
  get paid first; your common can be worth far less than the valuation implies.
  Ask the preference stack (1x non-participating is founder-friendly and common).
- **Exercise windows & the 90-day trap.** If you leave, you often have only 90
  days to exercise vested options — which can mean a large cash + tax bill to keep
  what you earned. Some firms extend to 7–10 years; that's a real, askable term.
- **83(b) election.** If you get *restricted stock* (not options) and can early-
  exercise, an 83(b) filed within 30 days can save enormous tax. Talk to a CPA —
  this deadline is unforgiving.

> Honest framing (repeat it to yourself): value options as a *positive-EV lottery
> ticket*, never as cash. Take an offer you'd still be happy with **if the equity
> went to zero**, then treat any upside as a bonus.

## 15. Relocation, Clearance Premiums & the Non-Cash Stack

The package is bigger than cash and equity. These levers cost the company little
and are routinely left on the table.

- **Relocation.** Lump sum (you keep the leftover) vs. managed move (they book it).
  Lump sum is usually better if you move cheaply. Always negotiable; often
  forgotten. For a move to a defense hub (El Segundo, Costa Mesa, Huntsville,
  Northern VA), ask for temporary housing and a cost-of-living adjustment.
- **Clearance as a line item.** An active, current clearance saves an employer
  6–18 months and tens of thousands in investigation cost. Name it:
  *"I'm bringing a current TS that's transferable — that's real schedule value to
  you."* For polygraph-gated IC work, the premium and onboarding delay are both
  larger; price both.
- **Skill premiums.** Niche, hard-to-hire skills (RF/EW, GNC, flight-certified
  embedded, safety-critical software) command above-band offers. Your
  PX4/Pixhawk + onboard-autonomy portfolio is exactly this kind of differentiator
  — see [12-career-software-engineering.md](12-software-engineering.md) and
  [18-career-resume-portfolio.md](18-resume-portfolio.md).
- **The quiet non-cash levers:** start date (buy yourself rest or a clean break),
  PTO, remote/hybrid days, a hardware/lab budget, conference and training budget,
  a defined promotion review at 6 months. Each compounds over years.

## 16. A Worked Example — From Recruiter Call to Signed Offer

A concrete end-to-end walkthrough so the sequence is muscle memory.

1. **Recruiter call.** They ask your number. You deflect (Section 13) and get the
   band: "Senior, base 165–195K, plus equity and ~12% bonus."
2. **Before the loop**, you set your three numbers: walk-away 200K TC, target
   245K TC, anchor 265K TC. You line up a second loop to land the same week.
3. **Loop goes well; you signal level.** In the hiring-manager chat you frame your
   PX4 autonomy stack and SITL test harness as Senior/Staff scope.
4. **Offer arrives in writing:** base 180K, 15% bonus, equity worth ~40K/yr over
   4 yrs, 10K sign-on. TC ≈ 247K. Solid, but you have a competing offer at 255K.
5. **You counter by email** (Section 5 template): enthusiasm first, then *"I have
   a competing offer at ~255K TC; you're my first choice because of the mission.
   If we can get base to 190K and the sign-on to 20K, I'll sign this week."*
6. **They come back:** base 188K, sign-on 18K, equity unchanged. TC ≈ 258K.
7. **You close:** *"That works — I'm in. Please send the updated letter and I'll
   sign."* You get it **in writing** before resigning anything.
8. **You document the brag doc** from day one and flag a level/refresh review for
   the next cycle (Section 8 / 14).

Net result of ~five emails: roughly +11K/yr base (which compounds into every
future raise and your next job's anchor) plus +8K up front — for one focused hour
of discomfort.
