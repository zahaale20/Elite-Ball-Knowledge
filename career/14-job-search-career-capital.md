# Job Search & Career Capital Strategy

> A standalone companion to [08-interview-prep.md](08-interview-prep.md),
> [09-resume-portfolio.md](09-resume-portfolio.md), and
> [06-negotiation-compensation.md](06-negotiation-compensation.md). Those files win
> the individual battles — the resume, the interview, the offer. This one is the
> *campaign*: how to think about your career as a portfolio of capital you compound
> over decades, and how to run a job search that actually works.

Most engineers run their careers reactively — they apply when miserable, take the
first decent offer, and repeat. The people who compound treat their career as an
asset under deliberate management: they know what capital they're building, they
search on their own schedule from strength, and they switch for *slope*, not just
for a raise.

---

## 1. Career Capital: The Thing You're Actually Accumulating

You are not "getting jobs." You are accumulating **career capital** — rare and
valuable skills, a reputation, a relationship network, and credentials — that you
later spend on autonomy, money, and impact. (This is the core idea of *So Good They
Can't Ignore You.*)

Four kinds of capital, all compounding:
- **Skill capital** — what you can do that few others can. The deepest moat.
- **Reputation capital** — what people believe you can do (see
  [13-personal-brand-public-presence.md](13-personal-brand-public-presence.md)).
- **Relationship capital** — who will vouch for and refer you (see
  [11-networking-mentors-sponsors.md](11-networking-mentors-sponsors.md)).
- **Credential capital** — clearances, degrees, shipped programs, named roles.

> Optimize early-career decisions for capital *slope* (how fast you're learning and
> building these), not for starting salary. Slope compounds; a starting number
> doesn't.

---

## 2. The Career-Decision Framework

When evaluating any role or move, score it on four axes — not just comp:

| Axis | The question | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|
| **Learning slope** | Will I be the dumbest person in a great room? | Skill capital |
| **Scope / autonomy** | Will I own something real and ship it? | Reputation + skill |
| **People** | Will I work with people I'll learn from and want in my network for 20 years? | Relationship capital |
| **Optionality** | Does this open doors or narrow them? | Future moves |

Comp matters, but a role that maxes comp and zeroes learning slope is a trap that
gets more expensive every year you stay. Early on, weight slope and people heavily.

---

## 3. When to Switch (and When to Stay)

- **Switch for slope, not for escape.** Leaving *toward* a steeper learning curve
  or bigger scope compounds; job-hopping to flee discomfort resets you.
- **The plateau signal:** when you've stopped learning and your next year looks
  like your last, the *opportunity cost* of staying exceeds the comfort of staying.
- **Tenure still matters.** A track record of 2–4 year stints with shipped impact
  reads far better than a string of 12-month jumps. Stay long enough to own
  outcomes, not just start them.
- **The internal move** is underrated — a new team, charter, or org inside a
  company you already trust can deliver the slope of a switch without resetting
  your reputation and network.

## 4. How Hiring Actually Works (the hidden market)

The posted-job application pile is the *worst* channel — high volume, low signal,
filtered by keyword-matching and luck. Most good roles are filled through:

1. **Referrals** — a trusted employee vouches; you skip the pile entirely. The
   single highest-yield channel. This is what your network (file 115) is *for*.
2. **Inbound from reputation** — recruiters and founders reach out because of your
   public work (file 117).
3. **Direct outreach to people, not portals** — a specific, credible message to an
   engineer or hiring manager on a team you've researched.
4. **Cold applications** — last resort; use only with a referral attached or a
   genuinely exceptional, targeted application.

The strategic implication: **invest in channels 1–2 during the years you don't need
a job**, so the search is short and from strength when you do.

---

## 5. Running a Search From Strength

- **Search while employed and content.** Desperation is detectable and destroys
  negotiating leverage. The best time to look is when you don't have to.
- **Run a pipeline, not a single bet.** Multiple live processes create real
  leverage (competing offers) and protect against any one falling through. Cluster
  them in time so offers arrive together.
- **Reverse the funnel** — start from "what do I want to build / learn / be paid"
  and target the 10–20 companies that fit, rather than spraying applications.
- **Target teams, not just companies.** Your manager and immediate team determine
  90% of your daily experience. A great team at an average company beats the
  reverse.
- **Time it for leverage** — concurrent offers are the only real source of
  negotiating power (this is the on-ramp to
  [06-negotiation-compensation.md](06-negotiation-compensation.md)).

---

## 6. The Application Mechanics That Move the Needle

- **Referral first, always.** Before applying anywhere, ask: who in my network
  touches this company? Spend the outreach to get the warm intro.
- **Tailor the top of the resume** to the specific role's core problem; the first
  six lines decide whether anyone reads the rest (see
  [09-resume-portfolio.md](09-resume-portfolio.md)).
- **A short, specific note to the hiring manager** outperforms a perfect portal
  submission — show you understand *their* problem.
- **Track everything** — a simple pipeline board (company, contact, stage, next
  action). Treat the search like the project it is.

---

## 7. Defense / Cleared-Career Strategy

The defense market has unique capital dynamics:

- **A clearance is portable, scarce capital.** Maintaining an active clearance is
  itself a career asset that opens a protected job market (see
  [07-security-clearance.md](07-security-clearance.md)). Factor "will this keep my
  clearance active" into moves.
- **Program experience is currency** — having shipped on a named program of record
  is a credential primes and startups both recruit for.
- **The startup ↔ prime ↔ lab triangle** — each builds different capital (speed and
  equity at startups; scale and process at primes; depth and publication at labs).
  A deliberate tour across them is a strong long-game.
- **Credibility in two worlds** (e.g., elite software *and* defense domain) is rare
  and the basis of the most valuable defense-tech careers.

---

## 8. The Long Game: Designing a Career, Not Drifting Through One

- **Run an annual review of yourself** — what capital did I build this year? Is my
  slope still steep? What's my BATNA (best alternative) if my job vanished
  tomorrow?
- **Keep your "story" current** — a living document of your wins, even when not
  searching. It makes any future search trivial and reveals capital gaps early.
- **Make reversible bets boldly, irreversible bets carefully.** Most career moves
  are reversible; treat them as experiments, not life sentences.
- **Define what "enough" and "winning" mean for you.** A career optimized purely on
  comp or title, with no destination, is how capable people end up successful and
  miserable (see [19-health-energy-career-longevity.md](19-health-energy-career-longevity.md)).

## ⚡ The Insider Layer — What the Field Knows but Rarely Writes Down

### The posting is often theater for a decision already made
A large share of senior roles are filled through the network *before* a public
posting exists, or are posted only to satisfy an internal/compliance requirement
after a preferred candidate has been identified. The application pile is the
slowest, lowest-yield channel — cold-apply-to-screen conversion sits in the low
single-digit percent, while a credible referral converts at multiples of that.
The practical consequence: spend your job-search hours *inverted* relative to
instinct. Most people spend 90% polishing applications into the void; the
operators spend 90% activating the warm graph (see
[11-networking-mentors-sponsors.md](11-networking-mentors-sponsors.md)) and 10%
on the pile. One good referral is worth a hundred cold submissions.

### Switch for slope, and know the loyalty tax
Reid Hoffman's *tours of duty* framing is right: think in two-to-four-year
missions, not lifetime employment. The financial mechanics the loyal rarely
compute: internal raises are typically capped at single-digit percentages, while
an external move can reset comp 20–40%+ because the market re-prices you and the
new employer pays to acquire you. Staying put is not neutral — it is a *loyalty
tax* you pay in foregone compensation. But "slope" is the real variable, not
salary: take the role where your *rate of learning and scope growth* is steepest,
because that's what compounds into the next three offers. A flat, well-paid role
is a slow leak of career capital.

### Run the search as a parallel funnel from employed strength
Never search from desperation or unemployment if you can avoid it — leverage
flows to the candidate who can walk away. Run **six to ten processes in
parallel**, deliberately, so they cluster into a tight offer window. This does
two things no single process can: it creates a real BATNA for negotiation
([06-negotiation-compensation.md](06-negotiation-compensation.md)), and it lets
you compress timelines — "I have offers landing Friday" is a legitimate,
powerful accelerant on every other process. Keep your current employer in the
dark until you hold a signed offer. And the counteroffer is usually a trap: the
majority who accept one leave within a year anyway, and you've permanently marked
yourself as a flight risk.

### Understand who the recruiter actually works for
Agency/external recruiters are typically paid a commission of roughly 15–25% of
your first-year base — which means they are motivated to *close a placement*, not
to optimize your long-term trajectory or your comp. Internal recruiters are
gatekeepers managing the company's funnel and budget bands. Neither is your
advocate, and that's fine once you stop expecting it: be honest about your
timeline, be easy to schedule, and use them for what they're good at — intel on
the band, the process, and the competing candidates. Use published comp data
(levels.fyi, Blind) to anchor the *band*, not a single number, and watch for
down-leveling on lateral moves — a title can rise while real scope falls.

### Tenure pattern is read as a risk signal
Recruiters and hiring managers pattern-match on your *sequence* of stints.
Three-plus jobs under ~18 months each starts reading as a flight risk regardless
of your reasons, and the burden shifts to you to explain it. Conversely, a single
endless tenure with no growth reads as comfortable and unambitious. The legible
shape is a few multi-year arcs each showing a clear *level-up*. Curate the story
so the trajectory — not the job-hopping — is what's salient.

### Build career capital before you trade on it
Cal Newport's *So Good They Can't Ignore You* names the deepest mechanic: rare
and valuable skills are the currency that buys autonomy, scope, and the right to
work on what you want. "Follow your passion" is backwards — you build the capital
first, then *trade* it for the mission and the freedom. And you don't need to be
top-1% at one thing: being top-25% in two or three *combinable* skills (Scott
Adams' skill stack) — say, controls + ML + the ability to write a crisp doc — is
rarer and more valuable than world-class narrowness, because the intersection has
almost no competition. Finally, the resume bullet that survives the ~6-second
scan has one shape: **did X, measured by Y, achieving Z.** Everything else is
noise the reader's eye skips.
