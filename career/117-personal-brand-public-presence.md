# Personal Brand & Public Presence

> A standalone companion to [115-networking-mentors-sponsors.md](115-networking-mentors-sponsors.md)
> and [18-resume-portfolio.md](18-resume-portfolio.md). Networking scales linearly
> with your time. A public body of work scales *while you sleep* — it does
> asynchronous networking, recruiting, and credibility-building for you, forever.
> This is how an ordinary person manufactures luck.

"Personal brand" sounds like influencer nonsense. Reframe it: a personal brand is
simply **what people reliably associate with your name when you're not in the
room.** You have one whether you cultivate it or not. The only question is whether
it's an accident or an asset. For an engineer, the highest-integrity version is
"the person whose public work is so good that opportunity comes inbound."

---

## 1. Why a Public Body of Work Is Asymmetric Leverage

- **Inbound beats outbound.** A strong public footprint flips the job search:
  recruiters, founders, and collaborators come to *you*, pre-sold. You negotiate
  from a position of being chosen, not choosing.
- **It compounds.** A post written once is read for years; each piece raises the
  baseline credibility of the next. The library is the asset, not any one book.
- **It's a credential you grant yourself.** You can't give yourself a degree or a
  title, but you can demonstrably *show the work*. In engineering, demonstrated
  ability outranks pedigree.
- **Asymmetric downside.** A thoughtful technical post has near-zero downside and a
  fat-tailed upside — one of them reaching the right person changes a career.

---

## 2. Pick a Lane (Positioning)

A brand that means everything means nothing. The goal is to be *the* person for
*one* legible thing, then expand.

- **Choose an intersection, not a single field.** "Autonomy engineer" is crowded;
  "GNC + sim-to-real for small UAS" is a category you can own. Specificity is
  memorability.
- **Anchor to what you genuinely do and care about** — a faked brand is
  exhausting and detectable. Authenticity is the only sustainable strategy.
- **Make it a sentence.** If someone can't describe your focus in one line, the
  brand isn't built yet. (See the "legibility" theme in
  [115-networking-mentors-sponsors.md](115-networking-mentors-sponsors.md).)

## 3. The Mediums, Ranked by ROI for Engineers

| Medium | Effort | Payoff | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Open-source / public projects** | High | Very high | The strongest possible proof — runnable, undeniable. This repo's `drone/` stack is exactly this. |
| **Technical writing (blog)** | Medium | High | Scales, compounds, demonstrates thinking. The default flywheel. |
| **Conference talks** | High | High | Concentrated credibility + in-person network. Defense conferences map to BD. |
| **Short-form (X/LinkedIn)** | Low | Medium | Distribution layer for the above; reach without depth alone is hollow. |
| **Long-form (papers, deep dives)** | Very high | High, narrow | Maximum credibility with the few who matter most. |

**The flywheel:** build something real → write about how it works → talk about
what you learned → the audience surfaces the next opportunity and the next thing to
build. Each turn is easier than the last.

---

## 4. What to Make

- **Show the work, not just the result.** The build log, the dead ends, the bug you
  chased for two days — process content is more credible and more useful than a
  polished "look what I made."
- **Teach what you just learned.** You don't need to be the world expert; you need
  to be one step ahead of the reader and honest about it. The "explained simply"
  post is the highest-utility form (see
  [116-technical-communication.md](116-technical-communication.md)).
- **Document your real projects.** A clear README, an architecture write-up, a
  "why I built it this way" post turns private work into public proof.
- **Have an opinion, defend it with reasoning.** Calibrated, well-argued takes
  travel; mush doesn't. State the tradeoff, not just the conclusion.

---

## 5. Consistency Beats Intensity

- **Ship on a sustainable cadence** — one good thing a month for two years beats
  ten things in one week and then silence. The algorithm and human memory both
  reward showing up.
- **Lower the activation energy.** Templates, a notes habit, a "things I learned
  this week" file you mine for posts. Make publishing cheap or it won't happen.
- **Done and public beats perfect and private.** Ship at 80%; the feedback is worth
  more than the missing 20%.

---

## 6. The GitHub / Portfolio as a Living Resume

- **Pin your best 3–5 repos**, each with a README that explains *what, why, and how
  to run it* in 60 seconds of reading.
- **Commit history tells a story** — steady, real work reads as competence.
- **A personal site** you own (not just platforms you rent) is the durable home
  base every other channel points to.
- **Connect it all** — site → GitHub → writing → talks, so one discovery leads to
  the whole body of work.

(See [18-resume-portfolio.md](18-resume-portfolio.md) for the resume mechanics this
feeds.)

## 7. Defense-Industry Nuance (the careful version)

This is the band where you must be most disciplined.

- **Classified and export-controlled work cannot be your portfolio.** Much of your
  best work may be invisible. Plan for it: keep an *unclassified* parallel body of
  work (personal projects, general techniques, open datasets).
- **Know the line cold** — ITAR/EAR, classification, NDAs, and OPSEC govern what
  you can publish. When unsure, don't post; ask security. (See
  [124-ethics-export-control.md](124-ethics-export-control.md) and
  [16-security-clearance.md](16-security-clearance.md).)
- **A public presence can be an OPSEC consideration** for cleared personnel —
  manage what's discoverable about you deliberately (see
  [36-trust-safety-opsec-and-digital-resilience.md](../information-environment/36-trust-safety-opsec-and-digital-resilience.md)).
- **Build reputation on general engineering excellence**, not program specifics.
  The principal who's publicly known for sim infra, estimation, or Rust never
  reveals a single program detail.

---

## 8. Failure Modes

- **All distribution, no substance** — hot takes with nothing built underneath.
  Detectable and fragile.
- **Perfectionism** — the post that's never good enough to publish.
- **Inconsistency** — three great months then a year of silence resets you.
- **Self-promotion that's all "me"** — generosity (teaching, crediting others,
  amplifying) builds more brand than bragging ever does.
- **Brand/skill mismatch** — a brand bigger than your ability is a debt that comes
  due in the first interview.

---

## 9. A 12-Month Plan

1. **Month 1:** Stand up a personal site + clean up GitHub. Pick your lane
   (one sentence).
2. **Months 2–6:** Publish one solid technical post per month from your real work.
   Cross-post to one distribution channel.
3. **Months 4–8:** Open-source one focused, genuinely useful project with an
   excellent README.
4. **Months 6–12:** Submit one talk to a meetup or conference in your lane.
5. **Throughout:** Run everything publishable through the export-control/OPSEC
   check first. Be generous; teach, credit, amplify.
