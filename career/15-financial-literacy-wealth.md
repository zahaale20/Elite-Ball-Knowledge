# Financial Literacy & Wealth Building for Engineers

> A standalone companion to [06-negotiation-compensation.md](06-negotiation-compensation.md).
> Negotiation maximizes the *inflow*. This file is about everything that happens
> *after* the money hits your account — turning a high income into actual wealth and
> freedom, which is a completely separate skill that high earners routinely fail at.

> **Disclaimer:** This is engineering-grade financial *literacy*, not personalized
> financial, tax, or legal advice. It teaches the mental models so you can ask the
> right questions and not get taken. For your specific situation — especially equity
> and taxes — consult a fiduciary advisor and a CPA.

The cruelest fact in personal finance: income and wealth are weakly correlated.
Engineers earning \$300k routinely have less net worth than disciplined teachers,
because wealth is built by the *gap* between earning and spending, invested over
time — not by the size of the paycheck. Master this and a defense-tech salary
becomes genuine freedom and optionality. Ignore it and it evaporates into lifestyle.

---

## 1. The Only Equation That Matters

$$\text{Wealth} = (\text{Income} - \text{Spending}) \times \text{Time} \times \text{Return}$$

Every lever lives in this equation:
- **Income** — maximized in [06-negotiation-compensation.md](06-negotiation-compensation.md).
- **Spending** — the lever you fully control and most people ignore. Your *savings
  rate* (the gap as a % of income) is the single biggest determinant of when you
  reach financial independence — more than your salary.
- **Time** — start now; compounding is brutally non-linear and unforgiving of
  delay. A decade of head start beats a higher return later.
- **Return** — mostly captured by being invested at all; chasing extra return is
  where people destroy capital.

---

## 2. Compounding: Why Starting Now Dominates

Compounding means returns earn returns. The consequence is violent asymmetry in
favor of time.

- **The Rule of 72:** money doubles in roughly $72 / r$ years at $r\%$ return. At
  7% real, ~10 years per double. The doublings that matter most are the *last*
  ones — which only happen if the early ones did.
- **The cost of waiting** is enormous: skipping investing in your 20s doesn't cost
  you those contributions, it costs you their final doublings — often the largest
  dollar amounts of your life.
- **Implication:** invest *early and automatically*, even small amounts, rather than
  waiting until you "have enough to bother."

---

## 3. The Defensive Foundation (do these first, in order)

1. **Emergency fund** — 3–6 months of expenses in cash/HYSA. This is what lets you
   take career risk (see [14-job-search-career-capital.md](14-job-search-career-capital.md))
   and walk away from a bad offer.
2. **Kill high-interest debt** — credit-card debt at 20%+ is a guaranteed negative
   return; nothing else competes with paying it off.
3. **Capture the full employer 401(k) match** — an instant 50–100% return; never
   leave it on the table.
4. **Insurance for catastrophes only** — health, disability (your earning power is
   your biggest asset), and term life *if* others depend on you. Insure what would
   ruin you; self-insure the rest.

---

## 4. The Growth Engine (boring on purpose)

For the vast majority of engineers, the winning strategy is *boring index
investing*, and the discipline to leave it alone.

- **Low-cost, broad index funds** (total market / S&P 500) beat the large majority
  of active managers over time, after fees. Fees are a guaranteed drag; minimize
  them ruthlessly.
- **Tax-advantaged accounts first** — 401(k), IRA/Roth, HSA (the most tax-advantaged
  account in existence — triple tax-free). Fill these before taxable brokerage.
- **Automate contributions** so investing happens before you can spend the money.
  Willpower is unreliable; automation is not.
- **Diversify; don't concentrate** — especially don't pile your taxable savings into
  your own employer's public stock when your *salary and equity are already* a
  concentrated bet on that company.
- **Time in the market beats timing the market.** You will not out-trade
  professionals; you don't need to. You need to stay invested through the scary
  parts.

## 5. Equity & Stock Comp: The Engineer's Wildcard

This is where the largest sums and the most expensive mistakes live. Pair with
§3–4 of [06-negotiation-compensation.md](06-negotiation-compensation.md).

- **RSUs** are cash that happens to arrive as stock — they're taxed as ordinary
  income at vesting. The disciplined default: **sell at vest and diversify**,
  unless you have a strong, specific reason to hold (and "I work here" is not one).
- **ISOs/NSOs (options)** are more complex: exercise cost, the spread, and **AMT**
  (alternative minimum tax) traps on ISOs can create a tax bill on *paper* gains
  you can't sell. Model the tax *before* exercising. This is where a CPA earns
  their fee many times over.
- **Concentration risk is the silent killer.** Your human capital (salary), your
  equity, and often your industry exposure are all bet on one company. A single
  position over ~10–20% of net worth deserves a deliberate diversification plan.
- **83(b) elections, exercise windows, AMT credits, QSBS** — these are real,
  high-stakes mechanics with deadlines. Learn the vocabulary so you know when to
  call the professional *before* the window closes.
- **Liquidity reality for startups** — private equity is illiquid and may never be
  worth anything. Never let illiquid paper wealth drive your spending or your
  diversification (it's a lottery ticket with positive EV, per file 15).

## 6. Taxes: The Largest Expense of Your Life

Over a career you'll pay more in taxes than for anything else. Treating tax as an
afterthought is leaving a fortune on the table — legally.

- **Marginal vs. effective rate** — a raise is never "taxed away"; only the top
  dollars hit the top bracket. Understand this before you fear a bracket.
- **Pre-tax vs. Roth** — pre-tax now (traditional 401k) vs. tax-free later (Roth).
  Roughly: Roth wins when you expect higher future rates; pre-tax wins when you
  expect lower. High earners often do both.
- **Long-term capital gains** (held >1 year) are taxed far lower than short-term —
  holding period materially changes your after-tax return.
- **Tax-loss harvesting, backdoor/mega-backdoor Roth, HSA** — legitimate tools high
  earners use. Learn they exist; have a CPA implement them.

---

## 7. Defense / Cleared-Specific Financial Notes

- **Financial responsibility is a clearance issue.** Significant debt, unpaid
  taxes, or financial mismanagement are among the *most common* reasons clearances
  are denied or revoked. Here, good personal finance is also *career security*
  (see [07-security-clearance.md](07-security-clearance.md)).
- **Report and stay current** — unfiled taxes or delinquent debt can end a cleared
  career. Boring financial diligence is a professional requirement, not just a
  personal virtue.
- **Foreign financial entanglements** can complicate clearances — be deliberate
  about foreign accounts and obligations.

---

## 8. Defining "Enough" — The Point of All of This

Money is a tool for buying time, security, and optionality — not a scoreboard.

- **Financial independence (FI)** ≈ when invested assets × a safe withdrawal rate
  (~4%/yr rule of thumb) covers your annual expenses. At that point work becomes a
  *choice*, which is the real prize.
- **Lifestyle inflation is the enemy of freedom.** Letting spending rise to meet
  every raise keeps the FI finish line running away from you forever. Bank most of
  each raise.
- **Buy back time and reduce stress, not status.** The research is consistent:
  beyond covering needs, money buys happiness mainly when it removes friction and
  buys freedom — not through consumption.
- **Wealth's real payoff is the autonomy to do work that matters** and to take the
  bold career bets in [14-job-search-career-capital.md](14-job-search-career-capital.md)
  and [17-engineer-to-founder.md](17-engineer-to-founder.md) from a position of
  safety.

## ⚡ The Insider Layer — What the Field Knows but Rarely Writes Down

> Still literacy, not advice — the disclaimer at the top of this file governs
> everything below. These are the mechanics so you can ask a CPA and a fiduciary
> the right questions.

### Savings *rate* dominates returns for the first decade
The single number that determines when you reach financial independence is your
savings rate, not your income or your portfolio's cleverness. The math is
stark: at a ~50% savings rate you reach work-optional in roughly $17$ years
*regardless of salary*, because a high rate simultaneously builds the pile faster
and shrinks the pile you need. A defense-tech salary with 10% saved is a slower
path to freedom than a teacher saving 40%. Early on, the contribution swamps the
compounding; the returns only take over the wheel after the pile is large. Fix
your spending the *day a raise is announced*, not after lifestyle creep has
quietly absorbed it — that's where the gap is won or lost.

### Equity literacy is where six-figure mistakes hide
The vocabulary that separates the informed from the wiped-out: ISOs vs NSOs vs
RSUs, strike price vs the 409A valuation, vesting cliff. Two traps recur every
cycle. First, the **AMT trap on ISO exercise**: exercising incentive stock
options creates a phantom "bargain element" that's taxable under the Alternative
Minimum Tax *even though you sold nothing and hold illiquid paper* — the classic
dot-com wipeout was engineers who exercised, owed cash on a paper gain, then
watched the stock crater while the tax bill stayed real. Second, the **83(b)
election**: on early-exercised or restricted stock you generally have *30 days*
to elect to be taxed now on a tiny value rather than later on the appreciation —
miss the window and you can be taxed at each vest on the full gain. These are
calendar deadlines with no mercy; this is exactly where you pay the CPA.

### Price startup equity like a lottery ticket
Most startup equity is worth zero, and even "good" exits can crush common
stockholders through **liquidation preferences** — a 1x participating preferred
stack means investors get their money back *plus* a share of the rest before
common sees a dollar. So negotiate the *base salary* hard (it's the part that's
real) and treat the options as an asymmetric ticket: bounded downside, lottery
upside, priced at roughly nothing in your planning. If the equity is the only
reason the offer is competitive, you are being paid in hope.

### Fees and behavior are the two silent wealth-killers
Over a 15-year horizon, low-cost index funds beat the large majority of active
managers (the SPIVA scorecards have shown this for years), and the reason is
mostly cost. A 1%-of-assets annual fee sounds trivial and is catastrophic: over a
full career it can quietly consume on the order of a quarter to a third of your
terminal wealth through lost compounding. The other killer is *you* — selling in
a panic at the bottom. The defense against your own psychology is automation:
automatic contributions, automatic rebalancing, and not checking the balance
during a crash. A roughly sensible order of operations for tax-advantaged space:
capture the full 401(k) *match* first (it's an instant ~50–100% return), then the
HSA (the only triple-tax-advantaged account), then Roth space, then taxable —
with attention to *asset location*.

### "F-you money" is a career instrument, not just a safety net
Three to twelve months of liquid expenses changes your *behavior*, which changes
your career. It's what lets you walk from a toxic manager, decline the
counteroffer, hold out for the role with the right slope, or take the founder
leap ([17-engineer-to-founder.md](17-engineer-to-founder.md)). Do not confuse net
worth with *accessible* runway — home equity and illiquid startup stock are not
months of rent. The freedom fund must be boring, liquid, and untouchable for
anything else.

### Financial discipline is also clearance security
The part that surprises people entering defense: per the published adjudicative
guidelines, *financial considerations* are among the most common reasons security
clearances are denied or revoked — unmanaged debt, unexplained affluence, a
pattern of irresponsibility. The same discipline that builds wealth also protects
your access and therefore your earning power in this field
([07-security-clearance.md](07-security-clearance.md)). Here, being boringly
solvent is a professional asset, not just a personal virtue.
